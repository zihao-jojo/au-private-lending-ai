## 1. Document Fraud in Australia

### 1.1 The Scale of the Problem

Document fraud in Australia has reached crisis proportions. The headline event: **Commonwealth Bank (CBA) discovered approximately $1 billion in suspected AI-assisted fraudulent home loans**, self-reporting to police and ASIC in February 2026. The fraud involved AI-generated payslips, falsified bank statements, draft tax returns submitted through accountants, shell companies, and deposits sourced from overseas channels to mask fund origins. ([CBA $1B AI Loan Fraud - ACS](https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2026/ai-heist--cba-calls-police-over--1b-loan-fraud.html); [UNSW Analysis](https://www.unsw.edu.au/newsroom/news/2026/03/commonwealth-bank-one-billion-dollars-suspected-loan-fraud-change-AI))

Key statistics:
- **Digital document forgeries surged 244% year-over-year** in 2024, surpassing physical counterfeits for the first time — now accounting for 57% of all document fraud ([Entrust 2025 Identity Fraud Report](https://www.entrust.com/company/newsroom/deepfake-attacks-strike-every-five-minutes-amid-244-surge-in-digital-document-forgeries))
- **1,600% surge in digital forgeries since 2021**
- **Deepfake attempts occur every 5 minutes** globally
- **Over 70,000 government-issued IDs** reported stolen or misused in Australia in 2025
- All four major banks (CBA, NAB, Westpac, ANZ) have flagged suspected bogus borrowing and are conducting comprehensive audits ([Switzer](https://switzer.com.au/cba-calls-in-the-cops-over-1-billion-of-suspected-ai-assisted-loan-fraud/))

### 1.2 AI-Generated Document Fraud Trends

The sophistication of document fraud has fundamentally shifted with generative AI:

- **AI-generated fakes** carry convincing formatting, authentic-looking logos, and metadata — making basic visual checks insufficient
- **Stolen data from large breaches** (Optus, Medibank, Latitude) can be pieced together into convincing fake identities, then leveraged through AI tools to create documents
- Criminals are using **mortgage broking and referral channels** as attack vectors
- **Traditional manual verification methods, outdated OCR systems, and static KYC/AML controls cannot detect these advanced forgeries** ([DoxAI](https://doxai.co/blog/ai-fraud-prevention-australia-2026/))
- The "Penthouse Syndicate" case (allegedly headed by former CBA and NAB banker Andrew Hu) illustrates the insider-knowledge dimension of these fraud operations

### 1.3 Industries Most Affected

| Industry | Fraud Type | Scale |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| **Lending (mortgage/personal)** | Fake payslips, bank statements, tax returns, synthetic IDs | $1B+ at CBA alone; industry-wide problem |
| **Rental/Property** | Fake income docs, employment letters, altered credit scores | 93.3% of landlords report experiencing document fraud; rental application fraud up 40% in 2024 ([Snappt](https://snappt.com/blog/tenant-fraud-statistics/)) |
| **Insurance** | Fabricated claims docs, staged accident evidence, fake invoices | $2.2B annual cost to industry |
| **Immigration** | Forged passports, fake bank statements, fraudulent English test scores | Dept of Home Affairs issued urgent Student Visa Integrity Alert in Nov 2024 ([VisaHQ](https://www.visahq.com/news/2025-11-19/au/home-affairs-issues-urgent-student-visa-integrity-alert-after-spike-in-document-fraud/)) |
| **Employment** | Fake qualifications, fabricated references, altered resumes | 42% of AU job seekers admit to lying; 85% of employers have caught CV lies ([Equifax](https://www.equifax.com.au/knowledge-hub/hr-solutions/resume-fraud-led-jail-time)) |

## 2. Insurance Fraud in Australia

### 2.1 Market Size of Insurance Fraud Losses

- **$2.2 billion/year**: Estimated total cost of insurance fraud to the Australian industry ([Insurance Council of Australia](https://insurancecouncil.com.au/consumers/insurance-fraud/))
- **$560 million**: Detected cases of opportunistic insurance fraud for motor and property alone in 2023 ([ICA](https://insurancecouncil.com.au/resource/insurance-industry-boosts-fight-against-fraud-and-scams/))
- **~$400 million/year**: Estimated undetected fraud cost
- **Australian general insurance market size**: ~USD $18.1 billion in 2025, projected to reach USD $33 billion by 2032 (CAGR 8.93%)
- **Global insurance fraud detection market**: $9.05 billion in 2025, projected to reach $22.14 billion by 2029 (CAGR 25.10%) ([Mordor Intelligence](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/insurance-fraud-detection-market))

### 2.2 How Insurers Currently Detect Fraud

Current approaches are largely manual and reactive:
- Claims adjusters reviewing documentation manually
- Special investigations units (SIUs) for flagged claims
- Rule-based alerts (e.g., claim value thresholds, repeat claimants)
- Voice analytics in call centres (emerging)
- Third-party data cross-referencing (limited)
- **Insurance Fraud Bureau of Australia (IFBA)**: ICA working element for information collection, sharing, and analysis

The industry has acknowledged these methods are insufficient. The ICA is actively working on "innovative fraud solutions."

### 2.3 AI/ML Fraud Detection Companies Serving AU Insurers

**Shift Technology** (France/Global) - Most Significant Development
- The ICA, Shift Technology, and EXL announced a **national data analytics fraud detection and investigations platform** for the Australian insurance industry in November 2025
- Developed with the ICA's **Insurance Crime Intelligence Network of Australia (ICINA)**
- Motor insurance claims as first focus area
- Platform build expected to begin early 2026
- Mirrors similar association-led initiatives in UK, France, Canada, Hong Kong, and Singapore
- ([Shift Technology](https://www.shift-technology.com/resources/news/insurance-council-of-australia-exl-and-shift-launch-new-collaboration-to-build-insurance-fraud-detection-and-investigations-platform); [ICA](https://insurancecouncil.com.au/resource/insurance-council-of-australia-exl-and-shift-launch-new-collaboration-to-build-insurance-fraud-detection-and-investigations-platform/))

**Gallagher Bassett** - Workers compensation focused
- Implementing data and analytics tools in fraud detection processes
- "Enhancing human judgment, not replacing it" approach
- ([Insurance Business](https://www.insurancebusinessmag.com/au/news/workers-compensation/gallagher-bassett-urges-industry-action-on-workers-compensation-fraud-557265.aspx))

**CLARA Analytics** (US)
- AI-based fraud detection for workers' compensation claims management
- Can detect patterns across entire treatment courses over multiple years
- ([CLARA](https://claraanalytics.com/news/groundbreaking-ai-based-fraud-detection-for-workers-compensation-claims/))

### 2.4 Specific Opportunity Areas by Insurance Line

| Line of Business | Fraud Type | Opportunity |
|-----------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Motor** | Staged accidents, exaggerated damage, phantom passengers, inflated repair invoices | ICA/Shift platform launching — but document verification within claims is underserved |
| **Property** | Inflated contents claims, staged burglaries/fires, fabricated receipts | Document forensics for invoices/receipts is a gap |

## 3. Identity Fraud in Australia

### 3.1 Statistics on Identity Fraud/Theft

From ABS Personal Fraud Survey 2023-24 ([ABS](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/people/crime-and-justice/personal-fraud/latest-release)):
- **255,100 persons (1.2%)** experienced identity theft in 2023-24
- **433,000 persons (2.0%)** experienced online impersonation
- **3.1% of people** were victims of scams (up from 2.5% the previous year)
- **1 in 4 Australians** will be a victim of identity theft at some point in their life
- **47 million data breaches** recorded in Australia in 2024 alone (~1 per second), ranking as 11th most affected country globally ([Privacy Australia](https://privacyaustralia.net/identity-theft-statistics/))

### 3.2 Cost of Identity Fraud

- **$3.1 billion/year**: Total cost to individuals and businesses ([AIC](https://www.aic.gov.au/publications/sb/sb42))
- **$1.6 billion/year**: Commonwealth Attorney General's Department estimate (majority: $900M lost by individuals through credit card fraud, identity theft, and scams)
- **$36,633**: Average self-reported cost per cybercrime incident in 2024-25 ([Cyber.gov.au](https://www.cyber.gov.au/about-us/view-all-content/reports-and-statistics/annual-cyber-threat-report-2024-2025))

Of those who experienced identity theft, stolen personal information was commonly used to:
- Obtain money from bank accounts, superannuation, or investments (25%)
- Open accounts in the person's name, including phone and utilities (12%)

### 3.3 Digital Identity Framework: ConnectID, myGovID, Digital ID Act 2024

**Digital ID Act 2024** ([Digital ID System](https://www.digitalidsystem.gov.au/what-is-digital-id/digital-id-act-2024)):
- Commenced **30 November 2024**
- Provides secure, convenient, voluntary ways to verify identity online with government and businesses
- Key safeguards: prohibition on single identifiers, prohibition on marketing use, restrictions on biometric collection
- **Phase 3** (by December 2026): myGovID (renamed to "myID") usable in private sector — bank accounts, electricity, telecommunications, leases

**ConnectID** ([Ashurst](https://www.ashurst.com/en/insights/australias-digital-id-act-and-a-new-trusted-exchange-tex-an-update-and-a-deep-dive/)):
- First private sector identity exchange to join AGDIS
- Backed by Australia's major banks
- Operated by Australian Payments Plus
- Accredited as identity exchange that securely transfers information
- Enables private sector identity verification without directly handling identity documents

**myGovID / myID**:
- Australian Government's Digital ID provider
- Transitioning from "myGovID" to "myID" to avoid confusion with myGov platform
- Currently used for government services; expanding to private sector by December 2026

## 4. Payment Fraud & Scams

### 4.1 Statistics on Payment Scams in Australia

**Record-level card fraud** ([AusPayNet](https://auspaynet.com.au/resources/fraud-statistics/2024-Calendar-year)):
- **$913 million** total card fraud on AU-issued cards in 2024 (up 20% YoY)
- **$816 million** (90% of all card fraud) was card-not-present (CNP) fraud
- **$454 million** overseas CNP fraud (up 25%)
- **$362 million** domestic CNP fraud (up 11%)
- Overseas CNP fraud rate is **12x the domestic rate** despite only 3% of card spend being overseas

**Broader scam losses**:
- **$2.03 billion**: Combined reported losses to scams in 2024 (down 25.9% from 2023, but still staggering) ([Scamwatch](https://www.scamwatch.gov.au/system/files/targeting-scams-report-2024.pdf))
- **$119 million+**: Losses in first 4 months of 2025 (up 28% vs same period 2024) ([BankInfoSecurity](https://www.bankinfosecurity.com/australian-scam-losses-increase-28-in-2025-a-28580))
- **$2.1 billion**: Card fraud losses specifically in FY2023-24 (up 9%) ([ABS](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/people/crime-and-justice/personal-fraud/latest-release))

### 4.2 Scams Prevention Framework Bill 2025

The **Scams Prevention Framework Bill** received assent on **20 February 2025** — creating the most comprehensive scam liability regime in Australian history ([Bird & Bird Explainer](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/australia/explainer-australias-new-scam-prevention-framework); [Parliament](https://www.aph.gov.au/Parliamentary_Business/Bills_Legislation/Bills_Search_Results/Result?bId=r7275)).

Key features:
- Amends the Competition and Consumer Act 2010
- **First sectors**: Banks, digital platforms (social media), and telecommunications providers
- **Maximum penalty**: **AU$50 million** in fines, plus liability to compensate victims
- ACCC designated as lead SPF Regulator; ASIC enforces banking sector SPF Code
- AFCA handles external dispute resolution from January 2027

### 4.3 Bank Obligations Under the SPF

Banks must meet obligations across six pillars ([Outseer](https://www.outseer.com/blog/australias-new-scam-prevention-framework); [Corrs](https://www.corrs.com.au/insights/the-new-scams-prevention-framework-key-considerations-for-regulated-entities)):

| Pillar | Key Requirements |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Prevent** | Targeted warnings for high-risk payments (especially to new/offshore accounts); payee-name checking; strengthened customer verification; brand protection against number spoofing/impersonation; tailored scam education; staff training |
| **Detect** | Shift from reactive to continuous, intelligence-driven monitoring; real-time or near-real-time transaction monitoring for scam behaviour indicators |
| **Disrupt** | Freeze/close scammer-controlled accounts quickly; immediate payment recall responses; customer-initiated account freezes via app/hotline; temporarily block suspicious transactions |
| **Respond** | Accessible internal dispute resolution (IDR) mechanism |
| **Report** | Intelligence sharing and reporting of scam outcomes (from end of 2027) |
| **Govern** | Governance frameworks for all of the above |

**Implementation Timeline**:
- **30 June 2026**: Banks comply with core obligations, including full IDR mechanism
- **1 September 2026**: All banks must be AFCA members for scam dispute resolution
- **1 January 2027**: AFCA begins hearing SPF-aligned complaints
- **End of 2027**: Intelligence sharing and outcome reporting requirements commence

### 4.4 Opportunities for Fraud Detection Tech

The SPF creates massive demand for:
1. **Real-time transaction monitoring** systems capable of detecting scam behavioural patterns
2. **Payee-name checking / confirmation of payee** technology
3. **Account verification** tools (CDR-powered, like Adatree's Insights product)
4. **Intelligence sharing platforms** between banks, telcos, and digital platforms
5. **Customer warning / intervention** systems (in-app, at point of payment)
6. **Scam pattern detection** using ML across multiple data sources
7. **Compliance management** platforms for SPF obligations tracking

## 5. Accounting & Tax Fraud

### 5.1 ATO Fraud Detection

The ATO uses **analytical models with behavioural and statistical algorithms** to analyse information on tax returns, BAS, and other tax forms, along with strong data- and intelligence-sharing capabilities with domestic and international partners ([ATO](https://www.ato.gov.au/about-ato/tax-avoidance/shadow-economy/tax-integrity-centre)).

### 5.2 Fake ABN / GST Fraud

**Operation Protego** — one of the largest fraud schemes in Australian history:
- **57,000+ people** allegedly committed GST refund fraud
- **~$2 billion** scammed from the Commonwealth
- Fraud involved individuals applying for ABNs to create fake businesses, then submitting false Business Activity Statements to claim dishonest GST refunds
- Often promoted through **social media networks**, with accounts claiming to be "financial advisors"
- As of July 2025: **126 people convicted**, with sentences up to **7 years and 6 months** imprisonment
- ATO investigated **150 of its own staff** for involvement
- ([ATO Operation Protego](https://www.ato.gov.au/about-ato/tax-avoidance/the-fight-against-tax-crime/our-focus/refund-fraud/gst-refund-fraud-attempts/operation-protego); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/business/568228/how-australia-s-tax-office-lost-billions-in-a-simple-scam))

### 5.3 Phoenix Company Activity

- **$4.89 billion to $5.1 billion/year**: Estimated economic impact of illegal phoenix activity ([ATO](https://www.ato.gov.au/about-ato/tax-avoidance/the-fight-against-tax-crime/our-focus/illegal-phoenix-activity))
- Prevalent in: **building/construction, labour hire, payroll services, security, cleaning, IT consulting, hospitality**
- ATO Phoenix Compliance Program has raised **$3.12 billion+ in liabilities** and returned **$1.35 billion+ to the community**
- **Phoenix Taskforce**: 38 Federal, State, and Territory government agencies (est. 2014) including ASIC, Attorney-General, Fair Work Ombudsman, Australian Border Force
- **Director Identification Number (DIN)** regime: Unique number for each company director requiring proof of identification, removing ability to use fictitious director names

### 5.4 Tech Addressing Accounting/Tax Fraud

Currently **extremely underserved** from a technology perspective:
- ATO relies on its own internal analytical models
- No significant private sector AI players specifically targeting AU tax fraud detection
- ABN verification is basic (ABR lookup)
- Phoenix activity detection relies largely on manual investigations and inter-agency data sharing
- **Opportunity**: Cross-referencing ABN/GST registration data with banking activity, employee declarations, and entity linkages using AI

## 7. Horizontal Fraud Detection Platform Opportunity

### 7.1 Vision: AU-Native Multi-Vertical Fraud Detection

A horizontal fraud detection platform serving multiple verticals would address the fragmented state of the market. The core insight: **the same AI-generated fake payslip used to fraudulently obtain a mortgage is also used to fraudulently obtain a rental property, inflate an insurance claim, or secure employment.**

### 7.2 Core Capabilities

```
                        +---------------------------+
                        | HORIZONTAL FRAUD PLATFORM |
                        +---------------------------+
                                    |
            +----------+----------+---------+----------+
            |          |          |         |          |
        Document   Identity   Financial  Entity     Risk
         Fraud      Verify    Verify    Linkage    Scoring
            |          |          |         |          |
        +---+---+  +---+---+  +--+--+  +--+---+  +--+---+
        |Payslip|  |ID Doc |  | CDR |  | ABN  |  |Cross |
        |BankSt |  |Liveness| |Open |  |ASIC  |  |Vert  |
        |TaxRtn |  |Face   |  |Bank |  |DIN   |  |Intel |
        |Invoice|  |DVS    |  |Txns |  |Entity|  |Score |
        |Certs  |  |Digital|  |Bal  |  |Graph |  |      |
        +-------+  |ID Act |  +-----+  +------+  +------+
                    +-------+
```

**Layer 1 — Document Fraud Detection (Core)**
- AI-powered analysis of AU-specific financial documents: payslips (PAYG), bank statements (AU bank formats), tax returns (ATO format), BAS, invoices
- 300+ forensic checks: metadata analysis, font consistency, pixel-level tampering, logo verification, format validation, cross-field consistency
- Training on AU-specific document templates, layouts, and regulatory formats
- Real-time detection of AI-generated documents (ChatGPT, Midjourney-style forgeries)

**Layer 2 — Identity Verification**
- Integration with AU Document Verification Service (DVS) via GBG/greenID or direct
- Digital ID Act compliance (ConnectID exchange integration)
- Biometric liveness detection (anti-deepfake)
- myID (formerly myGovID) integration as it expands to private sector (Phase 3, Dec 2026)

**Layer 3 — CDR/Open Banking Cross-Referencing (Key Differentiator)**
- **This is the moat.** Cross-reference submitted documents against CDR open banking data:
  - Payslip says income is $8,000/month? Check actual deposits in CDR bank data
  - Bank statement submitted as PDF? Compare against CDR-sourced actual transactions
  - ABN on invoice? Verify against ABR and check for CDR banking activity
- 99.7% of AU household banking market accessible via CDR (114 banking sources)
- Adatree's Insights product shows the way, but nobody combines this with document forensics
- CDR expanding to non-bank lenders from July 2026 — timing is perfect

**Layer 4 — Entity Linkage**
- ABN/ACN verification against Australian Business Register
- Director Identification Number (DIN) cross-referencing
- ASIC company data integration
- Entity relationship graphing (detect shell companies, phoenix activity)
- Beneficial ownership mapping

**Layer 5 — Cross-Vertical Intelligence**
- Fraud detected in one vertical informs risk scoring in others
- Network effects: the more verticals on the platform, the better the detection
- Anonymised fraud pattern sharing (similar to ICA/ICINA model but cross-industry)

### 7.3 Vertical Applications

**Vertical 1: Lending (Mortgage, Personal, Business)**
- Value prop: Detect fake payslips, bank statements, tax returns; cross-reference against CDR
- Buyer: Banks, non-bank lenders, mortgage aggregators, brokers
- Regulatory driver: Responsible lending obligations (NCCP Act), CBA $1B fraud wake-up call
- TAM driver: $224B+ AU private credit AUM
- Competitive positioning: Fortiro is closest competitor but lacks CDR integration

**Vertical 2: Rental / Property Management**
- Value prop: Verify tenant income and employment documents; detect fake references
- Buyer: Property managers, REAs, build-to-rent operators, institutional landlords
- Regulatory driver: State tenancy legislation; growing institutional rental market
- TAM driver: ~3 million rental properties in AU; 93% of landlords experience document fraud
- Competitive positioning: **Zero AU-specific solutions** — massive white space

**Vertical 3: Insurance (Claims)**
- Value prop: Detect fabricated invoices, receipts, medical documents within claims
- Buyer: General insurers, workers comp insurers, CTP insurers
- Regulatory driver: ICA fraud initiatives; SIRA enforcement
- TAM driver: $2.2B annual fraud cost; $18.1B general insurance market
- Competitive positioning: Shift/ICA platform focuses on claims patterns, not document forensics — complementary not competitive

**Vertical 4: Employment Screening**
- Value prop: Verify qualification certificates, employment letters, payslips for salary history
- Buyer: Employers, recruitment agencies, background check companies
- Regulatory driver: Fair Work Act; professional registration requirements
- TAM driver: 42% of job seekers admit to lying; fake qualifications scheme cases
- Competitive positioning: Current screening companies do database checks, not AI document forensics

**Vertical 5: Government / Immigration**
- Value prop: Detect forged passports, bank statements, English test scores in visa applications
- Buyer: Department of Home Affairs, education providers (Group of Eight universities targeted)
- Regulatory driver: Migration Act; Home Affairs integrity alerts
- Competitive positioning: Government procurement is slow but high-value; education providers are faster buyers

### 7.4 CDR Cross-Referencing as THE Differentiator

Why CDR integration is the strategic moat:

1. **Uniquely Australian**: CDR is an AU-specific regulatory framework — global competitors (Onfido, Jumio, Shift) cannot easily replicate this
2. **Source-of-truth data**: CDR data comes directly from banks via secure APIs — it cannot be forged like documents can
3. **Real-time verification**: Not relying on point-in-time documents but ongoing data feeds
4. **Expanding scope**: CDR rolling out to non-bank lenders (July 2026), energy, and telecommunications
5. **Low adoption currently**: Only ~530,000 consumers used CDR in H2 2024 — early mover advantage
6. **88% of lenders** are looking to use open banking for income verification ([The Adviser](https://www.theadviser.com.au/broker/48174-open-banking-pitched-as-antidote-to-ai-fuelled-loan-fraud))
7. **"Direct chain of trust"** between source accounts and credit decisions

The combination of **document forensics + CDR cross-referencing** creates a verification approach that is:
- Difficult for fraudsters to defeat (they'd need to fake both the document AND the banking data)
- More comprehensive than document forensics alone
- More actionable than CDR data alone (which requires context about what's being verified)

### 8.2 Addressable Market for a Horizontal Platform

**Global fraud detection market**: USD $32-59B in 2025, growing to $65-147B by 2030 (15-20% CAGR)

**Australia-specific TAM estimate** (fraud detection technology spend):
- Australia represents ~1.5-2% of global financial services (by GDP weight)
- Implies AU fraud detection TAM of ~$500M-$1.2B by 2025, growing to $1B-$2.5B by 2030
- **Serviceable addressable market** for a horizontal document fraud + CDR platform: ~$100-300M (lending + rental + insurance + employment verticals)

### 8.3 Why Now?

1. **CBA $1B fraud scandal** has made document fraud a board-level issue at every Australian financial institution
2. **AI-generated documents** have made traditional verification obsolete overnight (244% surge)
3. **Scams Prevention Framework** creates legal liability (AU$50M fines) that didn't exist before February 2025
4. **CDR maturation** makes cross-referencing technically feasible for the first time
5. **Digital ID Act** creates a standardised identity layer to build on
6. **All four major banks** are simultaneously auditing loan books and seeking solutions
7. **No incumbent** serves the horizontal opportunity — everyone is vertical-specific
8. **Fortiro proved the model** for lending — now extend it across verticals with CDR as the differentiator

### 8.4 Key Risks

- **Regulatory complexity**: CDR accreditation requirements; state-by-state tenancy laws; APRA expectations
- **Data privacy**: Handling sensitive identity and financial data under Privacy Act 1988 and CDR rules
- **Incumbent response**: Fortiro could expand horizontally; GBG could add document forensics; global players could localize
- **CDR adoption**: Consumer consent rates for CDR data sharing are still low (~530K users)
- **Government competition**: Digital ID Act expansion could reduce need for private verification in some use cases
- **Sales cycle**: Enterprise sales to banks and insurers are long (12-18 months)
