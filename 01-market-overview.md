# 1. Australian Private Credit Market Overview

## Market Size & Growth

| Year | AUM (A$ Billion) | YoY Growth |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| 2023 | ~$188B | ~7% |
| 2024 | ~$205B | ~9% |
| 2025 | ~$224B | ~9% |
| 2026 (projected) | ~$240-250B+ | Continued growth |

The A$224B market breaks down into:
- **A$132B** in corporate lending (~14% of total Australian lending)
- **A$92B** in commercial real estate lending (~18% of CRE lending domestically)

Broadridge projects Australian private credit investable fund AUM to grow at **22% CAGR**.

The alternative lending market is growing at **15.2% annually**, reaching A$23.07B by 2026, projected A$33.58B by 2029.

## Superannuation as Growth Engine

Private debt is the **fastest-growing component of private market assets in super**:
- Grown **75% in two years** to June 2024 (A$15B → A$27B)
- Allocation grew from 0.8% (FY2022) to 1.1% (FY2025)
- Additional **A$5.5B** expected to flow in FY2026 → total ~A$35.5B

Individual super fund allocations (June 2024):
- **Hostplus**: 2.8%
- **MLC Super Fund**: 2.7%
- **AustralianSuper**: 1.5% (A$7.2B)

## Why the Market Is Growing

1. **Bank retreat**: Major banks halved CRE exposure since 2009, creating A$50B+ funding gap in property development
2. **SME lending gap**: Big 4 approval rate only 25-35% for loans under A$1M; 1 in 5 SMEs had difficulty obtaining finance in 2025
3. **Institutional capital inflow**: Super funds, insurers, family offices attracted by risk-adjusted returns
4. **Elevated insolvencies**: More demand for alternative/rescue financing
5. **RBA rate cuts in 2025**: Improved borrowing capacity

## Market Consolidation Trends

- **Brookfield** acquired La Trobe Financial
- **Apollo** partnered with MaxCap
- **CapitaLand** acquired Wingate
- **Experian** acquired illion
- **AMP Bank** acquired Nano's home loan book

## RBA View

The RBA's October 2025 Financial Stability Review noted non-bank lenders are **"not yet sufficiently large to be of systemic importance"** but flagged indirect risk of banks lowering standards in response to competitive pressure.
